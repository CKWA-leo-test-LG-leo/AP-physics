## Topic 1: Kinematics

### 1.1 Scalars and Vectors

- **[Algebra]** Distinguish between scalars (distance, speed, time, mass) that have magnitude only and vectors (displacement, velocity, acceleration, force) that have both magnitude and direction
- **[Algebra]** Using positive and negative signs to indicate direction in one-dimensional motion, convention for choosing the positive direction
- **[Algebra]** Distinguish between distance (total path length, scalar) and displacement (directed segment from start to end, vector)
- **[Algebra]** Distinguish between speed (scalar, how fast) and velocity (vector, how fast and in which direction)

### 1.2 Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration

- **[Algebra]** Definition of average velocity — displacement divided by time interval
- **[Algebra]** Definition of average acceleration — change in velocity divided by time interval
- **[Algebra]** Three core relationships for uniformly accelerated linear motion (velocity-time, position-time, velocity-displacement)
- **[Algebra]** Free fall — all objects fall with the same gravitational acceleration when air resistance is neglected
- **[Calculus]** Instantaneous velocity as the derivative of position with respect to time, instantaneous acceleration as the derivative of velocity with respect to time
- **[Calculus]** Integrating acceleration with respect to time yields the velocity function; integrating velocity yields the position function
- **[Calculus]** Uniformly accelerated motion is a special case of the calculus framework where acceleration is constant

### 1.3 Kinematic Graph Analysis

- **[Algebra]** Slope of a position-time graph represents velocity
- **[Algebra]** Slope of a velocity-time graph represents acceleration; area under the curve represents displacement
- **[Algebra]** Area under an acceleration-time graph represents change in velocity
- **[Algebra]** Techniques for converting between the three types of graphs
- **[Calculus]** Slope corresponds to differentiation, area corresponds to integration — an application of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

### 1.4 Reference Frames and Relative Motion

- **[Algebra]** The same motion appears different when observed from different reference frames
- **[Algebra]** Vector addition of relative velocities in one and two dimensions

### 1.5 Two-Dimensional Motion

- **[Algebra]** Decomposing a vector into perpendicular components, recombining using the Pythagorean theorem
- **[Algebra]** Core principle of projectile motion — independent superposition of horizontal uniform motion and vertical free fall
- **[Algebra]** Key parameters of projectile motion: time of flight, horizontal range, maximum height
- **[Algebra]** The trajectory of a projectile is a parabola
- **[Calculus]** Horizontal acceleration is zero, vertical acceleration is gravitational acceleration; integrate twice each to obtain parametric equations
- **[Calculus]** Eliminating time from the parametric equations yields the trajectory equation

## Topic 2: Force and Translational Dynamics

### 2.1 Systems and Center of Mass

- **[Algebra]** A system is the object of study; center of mass is the average location of the system's mass distribution
- **[Algebra]** For discrete point masses, the center of mass coordinate equals the mass-weighted average of individual positions
- **[Calculus]** For continuous distributions, the center of mass is obtained by integrating the position vector over mass elements
- **[Calculus]** Three types of continuous distributions: linear density, surface density, and volume density

### 2.2 Forces and Free-Body Diagrams

- **[Algebra]** A force is a vector interaction between objects, measured in Newtons
- **[Algebra]** Procedure for drawing a free-body diagram — isolate the object and draw all external forces
- **[Algebra]** Common force types: gravity, normal force, tension, friction, spring force, applied force

### 2.3 Newton's Third Law

- **[Algebra]** Action and reaction forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects
- **[Algebra]** Common misconception analysis (interaction between person and ground, tug-of-war scenarios)

### 2.4 Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

- **[Algebra]** An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in uniform motion, unless acted upon by a net external force
- **[Algebra]** Mass is a measure of inertia — larger mass means harder to change motion

### 2.5 Newton's Second Law

- **[Algebra]** Net force equals mass times acceleration; acceleration direction is the same as net force direction
- **[Algebra]** Component form: resolve forces along coordinate axes and write equations separately
- **[Algebra]** Standard problem-solving procedure — draw FBD, choose coordinates, write equations, solve
- **[Calculus]** Momentum form of Newton's second law, applicable to variable-mass systems
- **[Calculus]** For variable forces, force is a function of time, acceleration is instantaneously determined, and the equation of motion is a second-order differential equation

### 2.6 Gravitation

- **[Algebra]** Near Earth's surface, weight is approximately mass times gravitational acceleration
- **[Algebra]** Universal gravitation is proportional to the product of masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance
- **[Algebra]** Variation of gravitational acceleration with altitude and depth
- **[Calculus]** Integral proof of the shell theorem — a spherical shell behaves like a point mass at its center for external points
- **[Calculus]** Gravitational acceleration as a function of distance, expressed piecewise inside and outside a sphere

### 2.7 Friction

- **[Algebra]** Static friction adjusts automatically to prevent relative motion, up to a maximum value
- **[Algebra]** Kinetic friction magnitude is proportional to the normal force, direction opposite to relative motion
- **[Algebra]** Friction analysis and inclined plane problems

### 2.8 Spring Force (Hooke's Law)

- **[Algebra]** The restoring force of a spring is proportional to displacement and opposite in direction to the deformation
- **[Algebra]** Equivalent spring constant for springs in series and in parallel

### 2.9 Uniform Circular Motion

- **[Algebra]** Centripetal acceleration is proportional to the square of speed and inversely proportional to radius
- **[Algebra]** Centripetal force is the radial component of the net force, not a new independent force
- **[Algebra]** Common circular motion scenarios (horizontal circle, vertical circle, conical pendulum)
- **[Calculus]** Differentiating the parametric equation of the position vector yields velocity and acceleration vector expressions
- **[Calculus]** Linear velocity is the cross product of angular velocity and position vector; centripetal acceleration is the cross product of angular velocity and linear velocity

### 2.10 Drag Force

- **[Calculus]** Linear drag model where drag force is proportional to velocity
- **[Calculus]** The equation of motion for an object under gravity and drag is a first-order linear differential equation
- **[Calculus]** Velocity increases exponentially from zero and approaches terminal velocity

## Topic 3: Work, Energy, and Power

### 3.1 Work

- **[Algebra]** Work done by a constant force equals force magnitude times displacement magnitude times the cosine of the angle between them
- **[Algebra]** Three cases: positive work (force and displacement in same direction), negative work (opposite), zero work (perpendicular)
- **[Algebra]** Net work done by all forces equals the algebraic sum of individual work
- **[Calculus]** Work done by a variable force equals the line integral of force along the path
- **[Calculus]** In one dimension, work equals the integral of force with respect to displacement

### 3.2 Kinetic Energy and the Work-Energy Theorem

- **[Algebra]** Kinetic energy is proportional to mass and the square of speed
- **[Algebra]** Net work done by all forces equals the change in kinetic energy
- **[Calculus]** Integral proof of the work-energy theorem starting from Newton's second law

### 3.3 Potential Energy

- **[Algebra]** Gravitational potential energy equals weight times height (with respect to a chosen zero reference)
- **[Algebra]** Elastic potential energy is proportional to the spring constant and the square of deformation
- **[Algebra]** Work done by a conservative force equals the negative change in potential energy
- **[Calculus]** Force is the negative gradient of potential energy — force points in the direction of steepest potential decrease
- **[Calculus]** Potential energy is obtained by integrating force along a path
- **[Calculus]** Universal gravitational potential energy varies with distance and is negative (bound state)

### 3.4 Conservation of Mechanical Energy

- **[Algebra]** When only conservative forces do work, the sum of kinetic and potential energy remains constant
- **[Algebra]** When non-conservative forces are present, mechanical energy is not conserved; work done by non-conservative forces equals the change in mechanical energy
- **[Calculus]** The time derivative of mechanical energy being zero is a first integral of the equation of motion

### 3.5 Power

- **[Algebra]** Power equals work divided by time
- **[Algebra]** Power also equals force times velocity
- **[Calculus]** Instantaneous power equals the dot product of force and velocity

## Topic 4: Linear Momentum

### 4.1 Momentum and Impulse

- **[Algebra]** Momentum is a vector, equal to mass times velocity
- **[Algebra]** Relationship between momentum and kinetic energy
- **[Algebra]** Impulse equals average force times time interval, and equals the change in momentum
- **[Calculus]** Impulse equals the integral of force with respect to time
- **[Calculus]** Momentum form of Newton's second law — force equals the time derivative of momentum

### 4.2 Conservation of Momentum

- **[Algebra]** When the net external force on a system is zero, total momentum is conserved
- **[Calculus]** Derivation of conservation condition from the center of mass theorem

### 4.3 Collisions

- **[Algebra]** Elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy
- **[Algebra]** Completely inelastic collisions result in objects sticking together, with maximum kinetic energy loss
- **[Algebra]** Inelastic collisions conserve momentum but partially lose kinetic energy
- **[Calculus]** In the center-of-mass reference frame, velocities of two objects reverse direction with unchanged magnitude after an elastic collision

### 4.4 Center of Mass Theorem

- **[Calculus]** The net external force on a system equals total mass times acceleration of the center of mass
- **[Calculus]** Total momentum of a system equals total mass times velocity of the center of mass

## Topic 5: Rotational Kinematics and Dynamics

### 5.1 Rotational Kinematics

- **[Algebra]** Radian measure is the natural unit for angles in physics
- **[Algebra]** Definitions of average angular velocity and average angular acceleration
- **[Algebra]** Three kinematic relationships for constant angular acceleration (completely analogous to translational forms)
- **[Calculus]** Instantaneous angular velocity is the derivative of angular position with respect to time; instantaneous angular acceleration is the derivative of angular velocity with respect to time
- **[Calculus]** Integrating angular acceleration yields angular velocity; integrating again yields angular position

### 5.2 Relationship Between Linear and Angular Quantities

- **[Algebra]** Arc length equals radius times angular displacement; linear speed equals radius times angular speed
- **[Algebra]** Tangential acceleration equals radius times angular acceleration; centripetal acceleration equals radius times angular speed squared
- **[Calculus]** Vector cross product expressions for linear velocity, tangential acceleration, and centripetal acceleration
- **[Calculus]** Differentiating the parametric equation of the position vector to obtain velocity and acceleration vectors

### 5.3 Torque

- **[Algebra]** Torque equals force times lever arm (perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action)
- **[Algebra]** Sign convention for torque (counterclockwise positive)
- **[Calculus]** Torque equals the cross product of position vector and force; direction given by the right-hand rule

### 5.4 Rotational Inertia (Moment of Inertia)

- **[Algebra]** Rotational inertia equals the sum of each particle's mass times the square of its distance from the axis
- **[Algebra]** Formulas for rotational inertia of common shapes
- **[Calculus]** Rotational inertia for continuous distributions is obtained by integrating the square of distance over mass elements
- **[Calculus]** Parallel axis theorem — rotational inertia about any axis parallel to the center-of-mass axis equals the inertia about the center-of-mass axis plus total mass times the square of the distance between axes
- **[Calculus]** Perpendicular axis theorem — for a thin plate, the rotational inertia about a perpendicular axis equals the sum of inertias about two perpendicular axes in the plane

### 5.5 Rotational Equilibrium and Dynamics

- **[Algebra]** Static equilibrium of a rigid body requires both zero net force and zero net torque
- **[Algebra]** Net torque equals rotational inertia times angular acceleration
- **[Algebra]** Massive pulley problems: tension differs on both sides; must simultaneously solve translational equation, rotational equation, and kinematic constraint
- **[Calculus]** Newton's second law for rotation is a second-order differential equation for angular position
- **[Calculus]** The restoring torque of a torsional pendulum is proportional to angular displacement

## Topic 6: Rotational Energy and Momentum

### 6.1 Rotational Kinetic Energy

- **[Algebra]** Rotational kinetic energy equals half the rotational inertia times angular speed squared
- **[Algebra]** Total kinetic energy equals translational kinetic energy of the center of mass plus rotational kinetic energy about the center of mass (König's theorem)

### 6.2 Work and Power in Rotation

- **[Algebra]** Work done by a constant torque equals torque times angular displacement
- **[Algebra]** Rotational power equals torque times angular velocity
- **[Calculus]** Work done by a variable torque equals the integral of torque with respect to angular displacement

### 6.3 Angular Momentum

- **[Algebra]** Angular momentum of a rigid body equals rotational inertia times angular velocity
- **[Algebra]** Angular momentum of a particle equals the distance from the reference point times momentum times the sine of the angle between them
- **[Algebra]** Impulse–angular momentum theorem — the impulse of net torque equals the change in angular momentum
- **[Calculus]** Angular momentum equals the cross product of position vector and linear momentum
- **[Calculus]** Relationship between angular momentum and areal velocity

### 6.4 Conservation of Angular Momentum

- **[Algebra]** When net torque is zero, the angular momentum of a system is conserved
- **[Algebra]** Applications — spinning chair, figure skater pulling arms in to spin faster
- **[Calculus]** Conservation of angular momentum is a vector conservation law; direction is also conserved

### 6.5 Pure Rolling (Rolling Without Slipping)

- **[Algebra]** Condition for rolling without slipping — center-of-mass speed equals radius times angular speed
- **[Algebra]** The instantaneous velocity of the contact point is zero during pure rolling (static friction does no work)
- **[Algebra]** Expression for total kinetic energy of a rolling object
- **[Calculus]** Deriving acceleration of a rolling object by differentiating energy with respect to time

### 6.6 Satellite Orbital Motion

- **[Algebra]** In a circular orbit, gravitational force provides the centripetal force
- **[Algebra]** Expressions for orbital speed, orbital period, and orbital energy
- **[Algebra]** Escape velocity equals the square root of 2 times circular orbit speed

## Topic 7: Oscillations

### 7.1 Simple Harmonic Motion and Restoring Force

- **[Algebra]** The restoring force in simple harmonic motion is proportional to displacement from equilibrium and opposite in direction
- **[Algebra]** Angular frequency is determined by the spring constant and mass; period is proportional to the square root of mass and inversely proportional to the square root of the spring constant
- **[Calculus]** The equation of motion for simple harmonic motion is a second-order homogeneous linear differential equation
- **[Calculus]** Solving the differential equation via the characteristic equation yields a cosine-form solution

### 7.2 Kinematics of Simple Harmonic Motion

- **[Algebra]** Position varies with time as a cosine function
- **[Algebra]** Phase relationships between velocity, acceleration, and position — velocity leads position by one-quarter period, acceleration is opposite in phase to position

### 7.3 Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

- **[Algebra]** Kinetic and elastic potential energy interconvert; total mechanical energy is conserved
- **[Algebra]** Total mechanical energy is proportional to the square of the amplitude
- **[Calculus]** Substituting the equation of motion directly verifies energy conservation

### 7.4 Simple Pendulum

- **[Algebra]** Under the small-angle approximation, the period of a simple pendulum depends only on length and gravitational acceleration, independent of mass and amplitude
- **[Calculus]** Deriving the pendulum's differential equation from the restoring torque and rotational law
- **[Calculus]** Linearizing the sine function under the small-angle approximation yields the simple harmonic motion equation

### 7.5 Physical Pendulum

- **[Calculus]** The period of oscillation for an arbitrarily shaped rigid body about a non-center-of-mass axis is determined by rotational inertia, mass, and distance from the center of mass to the pivot

### 7.6 Damped Oscillations

- **[Calculus]** Damping force causes exponential decay of amplitude
- **[Calculus]** The equation of motion is a second-order differential equation containing a first-derivative term
- **[Calculus]** Depending on the damping magnitude, the system is classified as underdamped, critically damped, or overdamped

### 7.7 Forced Oscillations and Resonance

- **[Calculus]** A periodically driven oscillating system
- **[Calculus]** Relationship between steady-state amplitude and driving frequency
- **[Calculus]** Amplitude reaches a maximum (resonance) when the driving frequency approaches the natural frequency

## Topic 8: Fluids

### 8.1 Density and Pressure

- **[Algebra]** Density is an intrinsic property of matter, representing mass per unit volume
- **[Algebra]** Pressure is the perpendicular force per unit area
- **[Algebra]** In a fluid, pressure increases linearly with depth
- **[Algebra]** Pascal's principle — pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally throughout
- **[Calculus]** Differential equation for pressure variation with depth

### 8.2 Buoyancy

- **[Algebra]** Archimedes' principle — a body immersed in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced
- **[Algebra]** Force analysis for floating, suspended, and sinking objects
- **[Calculus]** Deriving the buoyant force formula by integrating pressure over the surface

### 8.3 Fluid Flow

- **[Algebra]** Continuity equation for incompressible fluids — flow speed is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area
- **[Algebra]** Bernoulli's equation — along a streamline in an ideal fluid, energy is conserved

## Topic 9: Thermodynamics

### 9.1 Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium

- **[Algebra]** Temperature is a measure of hotness or coldness (macroscopic); microscopically, temperature is a measure of the average translational kinetic energy of molecules
- **[Algebra]** Zeroth law of thermodynamics — transitivity of thermal equilibrium
- **[Algebra]** Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin temperature scales and their conversions

### 9.2 Kinetic Molecular Theory

- **[Algebra]** Temperature is proportional to the average translational kinetic energy of molecules
- **[Algebra]** Root-mean-square speed is determined by temperature and molecular mass

### 9.3 Ideal Gases

- **[Algebra]** Ideal gas law — the relationship among pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of substance
- **[Algebra]** Standard conditions and the gas constant

### 9.4 Thermodynamic Processes

- **[Algebra]** Isothermal process — pressure is inversely proportional to volume
- **[Algebra]** Isochoric process — pressure is proportional to temperature
- **[Algebra]** Isobaric process — volume is proportional to temperature
- **[Algebra]** Adiabatic process — the product of pressure and volume raised to the power of γ is constant
- **[Calculus]** Work done by a gas equals the integral of pressure with respect to volume
- **[Calculus]** Differential derivation of the adiabatic process

### 9.5 First Law of Thermodynamics

- **[Algebra]** The change in internal energy equals the sum of heat added and work done on the system
- **[Algebra]** Analysis of internal energy, heat, and work in various thermodynamic processes
- **[Calculus]** Differential form of the first law of thermodynamics

### 9.6 Specific Heat and Heat Transfer

- **[Algebra]** Heat absorbed or released by an object is proportional to mass, specific heat capacity, and temperature change
- **[Algebra]** At thermal equilibrium, total heat absorbed equals total heat released by the system
- **[Algebra]** Latent heat — heat absorbed or released during a phase change

### 9.7 Entropy and the Second Law of Thermodynamics

- **[Algebra]** Entropy is a measure of the disorder of a system
- **[Algebra]** In an isolated system, entropy never decreases; natural processes have a direction
- **[Algebra]** In an isothermal process, entropy change equals heat divided by temperature
- **[Calculus]** Entropy change equals the integral of reversible heat transfer divided by temperature

### 9.8 Heat Engines and Efficiency

- **[Algebra]** Thermal efficiency equals net work divided by heat input, also equals one minus heat output divided by heat input
- **[Algebra]** Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible efficiency for an engine operating between two thermal reservoirs
- **[Algebra]** The area enclosed by a cyclic process on a pressure-volume diagram represents the net work

### 9.9 Third Law of Thermodynamics

- **[Algebra]** Absolute zero cannot be reached through a finite number of steps

## Topic 10: Electrostatics

### 10.1 Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law

- **[Algebra]** Like charges repel, opposite charges attract
- **[Algebra]** Coulomb's law — the electrostatic force is proportional to the product of the two charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance
- **[Calculus]** Vector integral form of electrostatic force for continuous charge distributions

### 10.2 Conservation of Charge and Charging Methods

- **[Algebra]** Charge can neither be created nor destroyed
- **[Algebra]** Three methods of charging: friction, contact, and induction

### 10.3 Electric Field

- **[Algebra]** An electric field is a force field that surrounds electric charges
- **[Algebra]** The electric field of a point charge is proportional to the charge and inversely proportional to the square of the distance
- **[Algebra]** Superposition of electric fields from multiple point charges
- **[Algebra]** Properties of electric field lines (direction, density, non-intersecting)
- **[Calculus]** Vector integral of electric field for continuous charge distributions
- **[Calculus]** Integral relationship between electric field and potential — the potential difference between two points equals the line integral of the electric field along the path
- **[Calculus]** The electric field equals the negative gradient of the electric potential

### 10.4 Electric Potential Energy

- **[Algebra]** The electric potential energy between two point charges is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the distance
- **[Algebra]** Work done by the electric field equals the decrease in electric potential energy

### 10.5 Electric Potential

- **[Algebra]** Electric potential equals the potential energy per unit charge
- **[Algebra]** The potential of a point charge is proportional to the charge and inversely proportional to the distance
- **[Algebra]** Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to electric field lines; potential decreases along the direction of the electric field
- **[Calculus]** Integral relationship between potential and field — the potential difference between two points equals the line integral of the electric field
- **[Calculus]** Differential relationship between potential and field — the electric field is the negative gradient of the potential

### 10.6 Capacitors

- **[Algebra]** Capacitance equals charge divided by voltage
- **[Algebra]** The capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor is proportional to plate area and inversely proportional to plate separation
- **[Algebra]** Energy stored in a capacitor equals half the capacitance times voltage squared
- **[Algebra]** Capacitors in series and parallel
- **[Calculus]** Deriving capacitance formulas for parallel-plate, spherical, and cylindrical capacitors by integrating Gauss's law

### 10.7 Conservation of Electrical Energy

- **[Algebra]** Work done by the electric field in a circuit equals the change in electric potential energy of the charges

### 10.8 Gauss's Law

- **[Calculus]** The electric flux through a closed surface equals the net charge enclosed divided by the permittivity of free space
- **[Calculus]** Choosing an appropriate Gaussian surface based on symmetry
- **[Calculus]** Applications to spherically symmetric, cylindrically symmetric, and plane-symmetric electric field calculations

### 10.9 Continuous Charge Distribution Calculations

- **[Calculus]** Three types of continuous distributions: line charge, surface charge, and volume charge
- **[Calculus]** Calculating the electric field of a continuous distribution via vector integration
- **[Calculus]** Calculating the electric potential of a continuous distribution via scalar integration

### 10.10 Conductors in Electrostatics

- **[Calculus]** At electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field inside a conductor is zero everywhere
- **[Calculus]** All net charge resides on the surface of a conductor
- **[Calculus]** The electric field just outside a conductor is perpendicular to the surface
- **[Calculus]** A conductor is an equipotential body

### 10.11 Dielectrics

- **[Calculus]** Dielectric polarization reduces the electric field strength
- **[Calculus]** The relative permittivity (dielectric constant) increases capacitance
- **[Calculus]** The electric displacement field and its Gauss's law

## Topic 11: Electric Circuits

### 11.1 Electric Current

- **[Algebra]** Electric current equals the amount of charge passing through a cross-section per unit time
- **[Algebra]** Conventional current direction is defined as the direction of positive charge flow
- **[Algebra]** Drift velocity of free electrons
- **[Calculus]** Current density vector — current per unit area
- **[Calculus]** Continuity equation for charge conservation

### 11.2 Resistance and Ohm's Law

- **[Algebra]** Resistance is proportional to resistivity and length, inversely proportional to cross-sectional area
- **[Algebra]** Ohm's law — voltage across a conductor is proportional to the current through it
- **[Algebra]** Three equivalent expressions for electrical power
- **[Algebra]** Joule's law — the heating effect of electric current
- **[Calculus]** Microscopic Ohm's law — current density is proportional to electric field strength

### 11.3 Series and Parallel Circuits

- **[Algebra]** In series: equivalent resistance equals sum of individual resistances; current is the same everywhere
- **[Algebra]** In parallel: reciprocal of equivalent resistance equals sum of reciprocals; voltage is the same across each branch
- **[Algebra]** Simplifying complex circuits and calculating equivalent resistance

### 11.4 Kirchhoff's Laws

- **[Algebra]** Kirchhoff's current law — the sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving it
- **[Algebra]** Kirchhoff's voltage law — the algebraic sum of voltages around any closed loop is zero

### 11.5 RC Circuits

- **[Algebra]** During charging, charge increases exponentially and approaches a maximum value
- **[Algebra]** During discharging, charge decays exponentially
- **[Algebra]** The time constant determines the speed of charging and discharging
- **[Calculus]** Setting up a first-order differential equation from Kirchhoff's loop rule
- **[Calculus]** Solving the differential equation to obtain the exponential charging and discharging processes

### 11.6 LR Circuits

- **[Calculus]** Self-inductance — a changing current in a coil induces an emf that opposes the change
- **[Calculus]** During current buildup, current increases exponentially and approaches a steady-state value
- **[Calculus]** During current decay, current decreases exponentially
- **[Calculus]** Energy stored in an inductor's magnetic field

### 11.7 LC Circuits

- **[Algebra]** In an LC circuit, charge and current oscillate sinusoidally over time, analogous to a mass-spring system
- **[Calculus]** An LC circuit consisting of an inductor and a capacitor produces electromagnetic oscillations
- **[Calculus]** The equation of motion is a second-order differential equation
- **[Calculus]** Charge and current vary sinusoidally with time
- **[Calculus]** Perfect analogy with a mass-spring system — inductance corresponds to mass, reciprocal of capacitance corresponds to spring constant

## Topic 12: Magnetic Fields

### 12.1 Basic Concepts of Magnetic Fields

- **[Algebra]** A magnetic field is a force field surrounding magnets and electric currents
- **[Algebra]** Magnetic field lines form closed loops (no starting or ending points), unlike electric field lines
- **[Algebra]** Magnetic poles always come in pairs (no magnetic monopoles have been found)
- **[Algebra]** Electric currents produce magnetic fields (Oersted's experiment)
- **[Algebra]** The magnetic field around a long straight current-carrying wire is proportional to current and inversely proportional to distance; direction is given by the right-hand rule

### 12.2 Lorentz Force

- **[Algebra]** The force on a moving charge in a magnetic field is proportional to charge, speed, and magnetic field strength, as well as the sine of the angle between velocity and field
- **[Algebra]** The Lorentz force is always perpendicular to velocity, so it does no work and cannot change a particle's kinetic energy
- **[Algebra]** A charged particle moving perpendicular to a magnetic field undergoes uniform circular motion
- **[Algebra]** Cyclotron radius, cyclotron period, and cyclotron frequency
- **[Algebra]** When velocity has a parallel component, the particle moves in a helical path
- **[Calculus]** Computing the three-dimensional Lorentz force using the determinant form of the cross product
- **[Calculus]** Velocity selector — a particle passes undeflected when electric and magnetic forces balance
- **[Calculus]** The cyclotron uses magnetic and electric fields to repeatedly accelerate charged particles

### 12.3 Ampere's Force (Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire)

- **[Algebra]** The force on a straight current-carrying wire in a magnetic field is proportional to current, wire length, and magnetic field strength
- **[Algebra]** Interaction between two parallel current-carrying wires (attraction for same direction, repulsion for opposite)
- **[Algebra]** Magnetic torque on a current-carrying loop (principle of the electric motor)
- **[Calculus]** Force on a general-shaped current-carrying wire in a non-uniform magnetic field requires a line integral

### 12.4 Biot-Savart Law

- **[Calculus]** A current element produces a magnetic field at a point in space proportional to current and element length, inversely proportional to the square of the distance
- **[Calculus]** The total magnetic field is obtained by integrating over all current elements
- **[Calculus]** Analogy with Coulomb's law — scalar charge corresponds to vector current element

### 12.5 Ampere's Law

- **[Calculus]** The line integral of the magnetic field around any closed path equals the net current passing through the surface bounded by the path times the permeability of free space
- **[Calculus]** Applied to current distributions with symmetry (infinite straight wire, solenoid, toroid)
- **[Calculus]** Comparison of Biot-Savart law and Ampere's law — the former is general but computationally intensive; the latter is simpler but only applicable to symmetric distributions

## Topic 13: Electromagnetic Induction

### 13.1 Magnetic Flux

- **[Algebra]** In a uniform magnetic field, magnetic flux through a plane equals magnetic field strength times area times the cosine of the angle between them
- **[Algebra]** Unit of magnetic flux: the Weber
- **[Calculus]** In a non-uniform magnetic field, magnetic flux equals the surface integral of magnetic field over the area

### 13.2 Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

- **[Algebra]** The induced emf equals the negative of the rate of change of magnetic flux (the negative sign represents Lenz's law)
- **[Algebra]** For a coil with multiple turns, the induced emf equals the single-turn value times the number of turns
- **[Algebra]** Three ways to change magnetic flux — changing magnetic field, changing area, changing angle
- **[Calculus]** Integral form of Faraday's law — the line integral of the induced electric field around a closed loop equals the negative of the rate of change of magnetic flux

### 13.3 Lenz's Law

- **[Algebra]** The direction of induced current always opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it
- **[Algebra]** The physical meaning of the negative sign in Faraday's law

### 13.4 Motional EMF

- **[Algebra]** When a conductor moves in a magnetic field, the induced emf equals magnetic field strength times conductor length times speed
- **[Algebra]** Two perspectives for derivation — Lorentz force and Faraday's law
- **[Calculus]** General form of motional emf — the line integral of the cross product of velocity and magnetic field along the conductor

### 13.5 Induced Electric Field

- **[Calculus]** A changing magnetic field produces an induced electric field, fundamentally different from electrostatic fields (non-conservative)
- **[Calculus]** The curl of the induced electric field equals the negative of the rate of change of magnetic field

### 13.6 Eddy Currents

- **[Calculus]** Closed loops of induced current form inside a bulk conductor in a changing magnetic field
- **[Calculus]** Eddy currents cause energy loss and the skin effect

### 13.7 Self-Inductance and Mutual Inductance

- **[Calculus]** Self-inductance equals the magnetic flux linkage of a coil divided by its current
- **[Calculus]** Self-induced emf is proportional to the rate of change of current
- **[Calculus]** Mutual inductance describes the electromagnetic coupling between two coils

### 13.8 Maxwell's Equations

- **[Calculus]** Gauss's law for electricity — electric flux equals net charge divided by permittivity
- **[Calculus]** Gauss's law for magnetism — magnetic flux is always zero (no magnetic monopoles)
- **[Calculus]** Faraday's law — a changing magnetic field produces an electric field
- **[Calculus]** Ampere-Maxwell law — both electric current and a changing electric field produce a magnetic field
- **[Calculus]** Displacement current makes Maxwell's equations self-consistent
- **[Calculus]** Maxwell's equations predict the existence of electromagnetic waves and yield the speed of light

- **[Algebra]** Distinction between specular reflection and diffuse reflection

## Topic 15: Waves, Sound, and Physical Optics

### 15.1 Basic Properties of Waves

- **[Algebra]** A wave is a disturbance that propagates energy without transporting matter
- **[Algebra]** Transverse waves (particle oscillation perpendicular to propagation direction) and longitudinal waves (particle oscillation parallel to propagation direction)
- **[Algebra]** Wave pulses and the principle of superposition

### 15.2 Periodic Waves

- **[Algebra]** Four basic parameters: period, frequency, wavelength, and amplitude
- **[Algebra]** Wave speed equals frequency times wavelength
- **[Algebra]** Mathematical description of waves
- **[Algebra]** Wave energy flux and intensity
- **[Algebra]** The speed of a wave on a string is determined by tension and linear mass density

### 15.3 Boundary Behavior and Polarization

- **[Algebra]** Waves undergo reflection and transmission at boundaries between media
- **[Algebra]** Polarization is a property unique to transverse waves
- **[Algebra]** Malus's law — the intensity of light passing through a polarizer is proportional to the square of the cosine of the angle between the polarization axis and the incident polarization direction

### 15.4 Electromagnetic Waves

- **[Algebra]** The electromagnetic spectrum — from radio waves to gamma rays
- **[Algebra]** Electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light in vacuum
- (The prediction of electromagnetic waves originates from Maxwell's equations, detailed in Topic 13.8)

### 15.5 Doppler Effect

- **[Algebra]** The observed frequency changes when the source and observer are in relative motion
- **[Algebra]** Frequency increases when approaching, decreases when receding
- **[Algebra]** Formula for the acoustic Doppler effect
- **[Calculus]** The electromagnetic Doppler effect differs from the acoustic case and must account for relativistic time dilation

### 15.6 Interference and Standing Waves

- **[Algebra]** Two coherent waves superpose to produce interference
- **[Algebra]** Standing waves are formed by the superposition of two coherent waves traveling in opposite directions, producing nodes and antinodes
- **[Algebra]** Resonance frequencies of strings and pipes
- **[Algebra]** Beat frequency phenomenon

### 15.7 Diffraction

- **[Algebra]** Waves bend when passing through an aperture
- **[Algebra]** The central bright fringe width in single-slit diffraction is proportional to wavelength and screen distance, and inversely proportional to slit width

### 15.8 Double-Slit Interference and Diffraction Gratings

- **[Algebra]** Young's double-slit interference — fringe spacing is proportional to wavelength and screen distance, and inversely proportional to slit separation
- **[Algebra]** Diffraction grating equation — grating spacing times the sine of the diffraction angle equals an integer multiple of wavelength
- **[Algebra]** Condition for bright fringes — path difference equals an integer multiple of wavelength; condition for dark fringes — path difference equals a half-integer multiple of wavelength

### 15.9 Thin-Film Interference

- **[Algebra]** Interference results from the path difference of light reflecting off the top and bottom surfaces of a thin film
- **[Algebra]** A half-wavelength phase shift occurs when light reflects from a medium of higher index of refraction

## Topic 16: Modern Physics

### 16.1 Wave-Particle Duality

- **[Algebra]** Light exhibits wave-like behavior in some experiments (interference, diffraction) and particle-like behavior in others (photoelectric effect, Compton scattering)
- **[Algebra]** De Broglie hypothesis — every moving particle is accompanied by a wave whose wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum
- **[Algebra]** Uncertainty principle — position and momentum cannot be simultaneously determined with arbitrary precision

### 16.2 Bohr Model of the Atom

- **[Algebra]** Electrons occupy specific energy levels without radiating energy
- **[Algebra]** When an atom transitions between energy levels, it absorbs or emits a photon of specific energy
- **[Algebra]** Hydrogen energy levels are inversely proportional to the square of the principal quantum number and are negative (bound states)
- **[Algebra]** Spectral series of hydrogen (Balmer series, Lyman series, etc.)

### 16.3 Emission and Absorption Spectra

- **[Algebra]** Atoms emit line spectra, which serve as elemental "fingerprints"
- **[Algebra]** The energy of an emitted or absorbed photon during a transition equals the energy difference between levels

### 16.4 Blackbody Radiation

- **[Algebra]** A blackbody is an ideal absorber and emitter of thermal radiation
- **[Algebra]** Planck's energy quantization hypothesis resolved the ultraviolet catastrophe

### 16.5 Photoelectric Effect

- **[Algebra]** The emission of electrons from a metal surface when illuminated by light
- **[Algebra]** The maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons equals the photon energy minus the work function
- **[Algebra]** There exists a cutoff frequency (below which no photoelectrons are emitted) and a stopping voltage

### 16.6 Compton Scattering

- **[Algebra]** The wavelength of X-rays increases after colliding with electrons
- **[Algebra]** The Compton effect provides evidence that photons carry momentum

### 16.7 Nuclear Physics

- **[Algebra]** The atomic nucleus consists of protons and neutrons; nuclear radius is proportional to the cube root of the mass number
- **[Algebra]** The strong nuclear force is a short-range attractive force that holds the nucleus together
- **[Algebra]** Mass defect — the mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of the masses of its constituent nucleons
- **[Algebra]** Binding energy equals mass defect times the speed of light squared

### 16.8 Radioactive Decay

- **[Algebra]** Three types of decay — alpha decay, beta decay, gamma decay
- **[Algebra]** Decay follows an exponential law
- **[Algebra]** Half-life is the time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay

### 16.9 Nuclear Reactions and Nuclear Energy Applications

- **[Algebra]** Nuclear fission — a heavy nucleus splits into lighter nuclei, releasing energy (nuclear power plants)
- **[Algebra]** Nuclear fusion — light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy (the Sun)
- **[Algebra]** Mass defect and mass-energy equivalence are the physical basis for the release of nuclear energy
